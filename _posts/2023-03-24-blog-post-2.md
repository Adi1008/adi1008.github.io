Imagine trying to predict the behavior of water molecules in a river. Following each individual molecule and calculating forces between every possible pair would quickly become an insurmountable computational nightmare. This is the fundamental challenge of bridging microscopic physics (with its countless degrees of freedom) to macroscopic phenomena we actually observe. While statistical mechanics can connect them for simple systems, it breaks down when there are more complicated interactions, especially when involving phase transitions. Field theory offers a beautiful alternative, shifting our perspective from tracking individual water droplets to observing the flowing patterns of the river itself. Instead of focusing on individual molecules, we use continuous fields, revealing emergent patterns that would otherwise remain hidden. This mathematical transformation from particle coordinates to flowing fields is done through the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation.

The partition function for a monatomic fluid in the canonical ensemble is given by:

$$Z_C = \frac{1}{n! \lambda_T^{3n}} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \exp(-\beta U(\mathbf{r}^n))$$

where $\lambda_T = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2\pi m k_B T}}$ is the thermal de Broglie wavelength.

If only pairwise interactions are important, then we can write the potential energy $U(\mathbf{r}^n)$ as:

$$U(\mathbf{r}^n) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^n \sum_{k=1 (k \neq j)}^n u(|\mathbf{r}_j - \mathbf{r}_k|)$$

This correctly accounts for double counting of "self-interactions." In order for the field theory to work, the pair potential must be "finite on contact," i.e., $\|u(0)\| \neq \infty$. It is straightforward to "regularize" some potentials. For example, the Lennard-Jones potential:

$$u(r) = 4\epsilon \left[ \left(\frac{r+\delta}{\sigma}\right)^{-12} - \left(\frac{r+\delta}{\sigma}\right)^{-6} \right]$$

If $\delta = 0.01\sigma$, then $u(0) = 4 \times 10^{24}\epsilon$, which is effectively infinite while being finite. Similarly, for a hard sphere potential, we say $u(r \leq R)$  is a big arbitrary number, but not $\infty$. In the following, we assume $u(r)$ has already been regularized.

Now, we rewrite Eq. 4.2 in terms of microscopic density operator: the sum of Dirac delta functions centered on the coordinates of each atom:

$$\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{j=1}^n \delta(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_j)$$

For any function $f(\mathbf{r})$,

$$\int f(\mathbf{r}) \delta(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_j) \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} = f(\mathbf{r}_j)$$

Using this property in reverse, we can rewrite the interaction between two particles:

$$u(|\mathbf{r}_j - \mathbf{r}_k|) = \iint \delta(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_j) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \delta(\mathbf{r}'-\mathbf{r}_k) \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}'$$

Summing over all particle pairs:

$$\sum_{j=1}^n \sum_{k=1 (k \neq j)}^n u(|\mathbf{r}_j - \mathbf{r}_k|) = \iint \sum_{j=1}^n \sum_{k=1 (k \neq j)}^n \delta(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_j) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \delta(\mathbf{r}'-\mathbf{r}_k) \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}'$$

When $j=k$, the double sum for just $j=k$ gives $n \cdot u(0)$. So, we subtract that out. And to prevent double counting (order of j and k doesn't matter), we get:

$$U(\mathbf{r}^n) = \frac{1}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}') - \frac{1}{2} n \cdot u(0)$$

## The Hubbard-Stratonovich Transformation

So, we can write Eq. 4.1 as:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \exp \left( -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}') \right)$$

where $\tilde{z}_0 = \exp(\frac{1}{2} \beta u(0))^n$ (the self-interactions do not depend on $\mathbf{r}$ or $\mathbf{r}'$).

The value of $u(0)$ only affects the reference chemical potential and has no thermodynamic consequences. The next step in the particle to field transformation is to invoke the definition of a delta functional:

$$\mathcal{F}[\rho] = \int \mathrm{d}\rho \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] F[\rho]$$

Just like a delta function picks out a single point in a continuous space, the delta functional picks out a specific function in a function space: $\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] = 0$ unless $\rho = \hat{\rho}$ at all points. The functional integral $\int \mathrm{d}\rho$ integrates over all possible configurations of the field. The delta functional ensures that the integrand is non-zero only when $\rho = \hat{\rho}$. So, every value of $F[\rho \neq \hat{\rho}]$ is multiplied by 0, and the result is $F[\hat{\rho}]$.

This allows us to introduce a continuous density field $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ while ensuring it matches the actual microscopic particle density $\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})$.

Now, let us write $\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}]$ in a way that will make future calculations easier. Imagine we have a cubic box containing our fluid with side length L. We divide this cube into a 3D grid of smaller cubes (N_g total grid points, each cube = "grid point"). At each grid point, we assign values for $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ and $w(\mathbf{r})$, which represent the particle density and auxiliary field (local chemical potential), respectively. By discretizing space into grid points, we convert the abstract concept of functional integration into something more concrete:

$$\int \mathrm{d}\rho \approx \int \mathrm{d}\rho(\mathbf{r}_1) \int \mathrm{d}\rho(\mathbf{r}_2) \cdots \int \mathrm{d}\rho(\mathbf{r}_{N_g})$$

Going back to the delta functional $\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}]$, we can approximate this as a product of delta functions at each grid point in 3D space:

$$\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] \approx \prod_j \delta(\rho(\mathbf{r}_j) - \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}_j))$$

For a single delta function, its Fourier representation is:

$$\delta(\rho(\mathbf{r}) - \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{iw(\mathbf{r})[\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})]} dw(\mathbf{r})$$

where $w$ is the Fourier variable, interpreted physically as a fluctuating chemical potential field.

Since the delta functional is the product of the delta functions at each grid point:

$$\delta[\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] \approx \prod_j \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{iw(\mathbf{r}_j)[\rho(\mathbf{r}_j)-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}_j)]} dw(\mathbf{r}_j)$$

There are $N_g$ grid points, and each contributes $\frac{1}{2\pi}$, so:

$$\approx \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{N_g}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dw_1 \cdots dw_{N_g} \prod_j e^{iw_j[\rho_j-\hat{\rho}_j]}$$

$$= \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{N_g}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dw_1 \cdots dw_{N_g} \exp \left[ i \sum_{j=1}^{N_g} w_j [\rho_j-\hat{\rho}_j] \right]$$

When we take the limit as $N_g \to \infty$, the spacing between grid points approaches 0. So, the sum in the argument of the exponential becomes:

$$\sum_{j=1}^{N_g} w_j [\rho_j-\hat{\rho}_j] \longrightarrow \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r}) [\rho(\mathbf{r}) - \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})]$$

Integrating over discretized variables becomes continuous:

$$\delta[\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] \approx \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{N_g}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dw_1 \cdots dw_{N_g} \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r}) [\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] \right]$$

Imagine we have infinite grid points, and at each grid point, we can vary the value of the auxiliary field freely. This is just a functional integral! What we have gone through can be viewed as the formal definition of the functional integral:

$$\delta[\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] = \int Dw \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r}) [\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] \right]$$

Now, we go back to Eq. 4.6, the partition function $Z_C$ that involves the microscopic density $\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})$. Our goal is to transform this into a field theory by introducing an auxiliary field $w(\mathbf{r})$. First, we know $\int \mathrm{d}\rho \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] = 1$. So, we can insert it into Eq. 4.6 without changing its value:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \int \mathrm{d}\rho \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] \exp \left[ -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}') \right]$$

Now, we rearrange the order of integration:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] \exp \left[ -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r}') \right]$$

The delta functional $\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}]$ constrains $\rho=\hat{\rho}$. For an arbitrary functional, $\delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}]G[\hat{\rho}] = \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}]G[\rho]$. So, we can replace the $\hat{\rho}$ with $\rho$ in the exponential:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \delta[\rho-\hat{\rho}] \exp \left[ -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') \right]$$

Now, we plug in Eq. 4.8 for the delta functional in Eq. 4.9, which gives us:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int Dw \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})[\rho(\mathbf{r})-\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})] -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') \right]$$

It is important to note that as a result of these transformations, the only factor in the integrand that depends on the atomic coordinates is $\exp[-i\int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})]$. The integrals over the $n$ particle positions thus factor according to:

$$\int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n e^{-i\int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})} = \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^n e^{-i\sum_{j=1}^n w(\mathbf{r}_j)}$$

The integral is taken over the volume of the system:

$$= \prod_{j=1}^n \int_V \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}_j e^{-iw(\mathbf{r}_j)}$$

Since each particle's position is independent, we can separate the 3N dimensional integral into a product of N 3D integrals:

$$= \prod_{j=1}^n V \cdot \frac{1}{V} \int_V \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}_j e^{-iw(\mathbf{r}_j)} \Rightarrow \text{let this be } Q[iw]$$

$$= \prod_{j=1}^n V \cdot Q[iw]$$

$$= (V \cdot Q[iw])^n$$

The functional $Q[iw]$ can be interpreted as a single-particle partition function, i.e., the contribution to the partition function from an atom that interacts not with other atoms, but only with the purely imaginary field $iw(\mathbf{r})$. Much easier to compute for an atom than a polymer.

Upon combining Eq. 4.10 and 4.11, the particle to field transformation is complete. Substituting Eq. 4.11 into 4.10, we get:

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int Dw \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\rho(\mathbf{r}) -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') \right] (VQ[iw])^n$$

We can write $(VQ[iw])^n$ using logarithms:

$$(VQ[iw])^n = e^{n \ln(VQ)}$$

$$Z_C = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int Dw \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\rho(\mathbf{r}) -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') + n \ln(VQ[iw]) \right]$$

The total volume V doesn't depend on the positions of the particles. We can pull it out of the integral and define a new constant $Z_0 = \frac{\lambda_T^{-3n}}{n!} (V)^n$:

$$Z_C = Z_0 \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int Dw \exp \left[ i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\rho(\mathbf{r}) -\frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') + n \ln(Q[iw]) \right]$$

Overall, we can write this as:

$$Z_C(n,V,T) = Z_0 \int \mathrm{d}\rho \int Dw \exp[-\mathcal{H}]$$

where the functional:

$$\mathcal{H} = -i \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} w(\mathbf{r})\rho(\mathbf{r}) + \frac{\beta}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r} \int \mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}' \rho(\mathbf{r}) u(|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|) \rho(\mathbf{r}') - n \ln(Q[iw])$$

is referred to as an "effective Hamiltonian" or "action". Very remarkable result of this transformation: a canonical partition function for a monatomic fluid with pairwise interactions described by an arbitrary pair potential $u(r)$ can be expressed as a statistical field theory.

The partition function is proportional to a functional integral over two fluctuating fields, $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ and $w(\mathbf{r})$:

1. $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ is a fluctuating particle number density, since it was constrained to be microscopically identical to the density $\hat{\rho}(\mathbf{r})$.
2. $w(\mathbf{r})$ is a fluctuating inhomogeneous chemical potential field, because iw appears as the thermodynamically conjugate variable to the field $\rho$ in the "chemical potential" term of the effective Hamiltonian $\mathcal{H}$. Note: the equilibrium chemical potential of a fluid, $\mu$, is spatially uniform, and is NOT $w(\mathbf{r})$.

## Intuitive Understanding

The Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation is a mathematical technique that allows us to transform a many-body problem of interacting particles into a problem involving fields. This is analogous to how electromagnetism can be formulated either in terms of charged particles directly interacting with each other, or in terms of charged particles interacting with electromagnetic fields.

The key insight is that we can introduce auxiliary fields ($\rho$ and $w$) that capture the collective behavior of the system. The density field $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ represents how particles are distributed in space, while the field $w(\mathbf{r})$ can be thought of as a chemical potential field that influences where particles want to be.

By transforming from particle coordinates to fields, we've made the problem more tractable for certain types of analysis, especially when dealing with complex systems like polymers. The formulation in terms of fields is particularly useful for studying phase transitions, inhomogeneous systems, and applying renormalization group techniques.
